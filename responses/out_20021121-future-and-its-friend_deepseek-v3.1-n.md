# The Future and Its Friends (November 2002)

## 1. SUMMARY

The article discusses Freeman Dyson's review of Timothy Ferris's book "Seeing in the Dark," which explores how amateur astronomers are increasingly able to contribute meaningfully to scientific discovery. Dyson proposes that sciences evolve through three phases: a Baconian phase (fact-gathering dominated by amateurs), a Cartesian phase (precise measurements and quantitative theories dominated by professionals), and finally a hybrid phase where cheap, powerful tools empower both amateurs and professionals.

The author applies this framework to biology, arguing it's the next science ready for transformation. High school students were already performing experiments (like PCR) that would have been unthinkable in the 1970s, with molecular biology supply companies making tools increasingly accessible. Dyson envisioned "legions of homegrown DNA tinkerers," a prospect the author finds both "thrilling and alarming."

## 2. HISTORY

The prediction that biology would enter its third phase with widespread amateur participation proved remarkably prescient, though the specific trajectory differed from expectations. Several concrete developments emerged:

**DIY Biology Movement**: The mid-to-late 2000s saw the emergence of biohacking and DIY biology communities. The first community biology lab opened in 2010 (Genspace in Brooklyn), followed by dozens of others globally. These spaces enabled amateur biologists to work with DNA sequencing, CRISPR, and synthetic biology tools.

**Commercial DNA Technology**: Personal genomics exploded with 23andMe launching in 2006 and dropping prices from $999 to $99 by 2012. By 2020, over 26 million people had taken consumer DNA tests. Sequencing costs plummeted from billions (Human Genome Project, 2003) to under $1000 by 2015 and under $100 by 2022.

**CRISPR Revolution**: The 2012 discovery of CRISPR-Cas9 as a gene-editing tool democratized genetic engineering. Jennifer Doudna and Emmanuelle Charpentier won the 2020 Nobel Prize for this work. CRISPR kits became commercially available for education and amateur use, though with regulatory restrictions.

**Regulatory Response**: The DIY biology movement prompted policy attention. The FBI began engaging with biohacker communities around 2009, focusing on security concerns. Regulations tightened around synthetic DNA due to biosecurity worries.

**Limited Scientific Impact**: Despite accessibility, amateur biologists haven't produced major breakthroughs comparable to professional research. Most significant biological discoveries still require institutional resources, specialized expertise, and regulatory compliance. The democratization occurred more in education and personal genomics than in cutting-edge research contributions.
