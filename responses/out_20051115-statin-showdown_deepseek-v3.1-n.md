# Statin Showdown (November 2005)

## 1. SUMMARY

This article analyzes the results from the IDEAL trial presented at the American Heart Association meeting, which compared Pfizer's Lipitor (atorvastatin) against Merck's Zocor (simvastatin) in 8,800 patients who had previously suffered heart attacks. The trial ran for nearly five years with one group receiving high-dose Lipitor and the other receiving standard-dose Zocor. 

The key finding was that while Lipitor demonstrated superior LDL-lowering effects, it failed to show a statistically significant advantage in the primary endpoint of "major coronary events." Lipitor showed some benefits in secondary endpoints like nonfatal heart attacks, but overall cardiovascular mortality was identical between both groups. The article notes this was problematic for Pfizer because Zocor was approaching patent expiration, making it likely that HMOs would switch patients to the cheaper generic simvastatin. The author also mentions this casts doubt on the "lower LDL is always better" hypothesis that Pfizer had been promoting.

## 2. HISTORY

The subsequent history confirmed several key aspects from this 2005 analysis:

The ACCORD-Lipid trial (2010) examined whether adding fenofibrate to simvastatin therapy would benefit high-risk diabetic patients but found no significant cardiovascular benefit, reinforcing that lipid management alone wasn't always sufficient.

The SHARP trial (2011) examined simvastatin plus ezetimibe in chronic kidney disease patients and showed meaningful cardiovascular risk reduction, providing support for combination therapy approaches.

Vytorin's outcomes proved complex - while it effectively lowered LDL cholesterol more than statin monotherapy, the IMPROVE-IT trial (2015) showed only modest additional cardiovascular benefit over simvastatin alone, with questions raised about whether the small benefit justified the increased cost.

Generic atorvastatin became available in 2011, fundamentally altering the economic landscape and reducing the relevance of many earlier comparative effectiveness debates.

The **LDL hypothesis ultimately proved more robust than suggested in 2005** - subsequent trials like FOURIER (2017) with PCSK9 inhibitors demonstrated that dramatic LDL reduction does produce substantial cardiovascular benefit, suggesting the issue in 2005 was insufficient statistical power and trial design rather than an invalid hypothesis.
