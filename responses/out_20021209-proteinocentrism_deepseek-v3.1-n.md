# Proteinocentrism (December 2002)

## 1. SUMMARY

The article argues that the scientific community focuses disproportionately on proteins and nucleic acids due to technical accessibility rather than biological importance. The author explains that DNA/RNA sequencing became highly automated because these molecules use only a few building blocks (five bases and two sugars). Proteins, with their twenty amino acids, are more complex but still manageable due to their linear structure and relative stability.

In contrast, lipids and carbohydrates receive far less attention despite their biological significance because they are technically challenging to work with. Lipids exist in hundreds of varieties with different chain lengths, saturations, and modifications, are often present at low concentrations, and have poor solubility. Carbohydrates are even worse—they branch extensively, lack a general automated sequencing method, and have difficult physical properties for purification.

The author concludes that this technical bias creates blind spots in understanding hybrid molecules like glycosylated proteins and lipid-protein complexes, leaving important areas of biology underexplored.

## 2. HISTORY

The author's core insight proved remarkably prescient. In the two decades following this article, glycomics and lipidomics emerged as major fields addressing exactly these neglected biomolecule classes:

**Carbohydrates/Glycomics:**
- Automated glycan sequencing technologies gradually improved, though still lag behind nucleic acid and protein methods in throughput and standardization
- Glycobiology gained recognition as critical to therapeutics—biologics (monoclonal antibodies, fusion proteins) routinely require glycan analysis and engineering for efficacy, pharmacokinetics, and immunogenicity
- Glycosylation became a key manufacturing consideration for FDA-approved biologics, with specific glycoforms associated with different clinical outcomes
- Technologies like liquid chromatography-mass spectrometry (LC-MS) for glycomics and tools for glycosylation site mapping became more accessible, though the field remains technically demanding

**Lipids/Lipidomics:**
- Mass spectrometry-based lipidomics developed into a robust field, enabling comprehensive lipid profiling and discovery
- Lipids gained recognition in cell signaling (phosphoinositides, sphingolipids, eicosanoids), membrane biology, and metabolic regulation
- Lipid-based therapeutics expanded, including lipid nanoparticles (crucial for mRNA COVID vaccines), omega-3 fatty acids, and lipid-lowering drugs

**Technical Progress:**
- Multi-omics approaches integrating genomics, proteomics, metabolomics, lipidomics, and glycomics became standard for systems biology
- Cryo-electron microscopy revolutionized structural biology of complexes involving proteins, nucleic acids, and lipids
- Glycoengineering of therapeutic proteins became routine in biopharmaceutical manufacturing

**Commercial/Clinical Impact:**
- Numerous FDA-approved biologics require glycan analysis (e.g., antibody-drug conjugates, erythropoietin, clotting factors)
- Lipid nanoparticle delivery systems enabled mRNA vaccines and therapies
- However, the complexity gap persists—glycan and lipid analysis still requires specialized expertise and instrumentation
