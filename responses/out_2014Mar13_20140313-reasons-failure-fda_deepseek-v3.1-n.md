# Retrospective Analysis of "The Reasons for Failure at the FDA" (Science Magazine, 2014)

## 1. SUMMARY

The 2014 Science Magazine article examined FDA rejection patterns for New Molecular Entities (NMEs) from 2000-2012, drawing from a JAMA retrospective study. The key findings revealed that roughly 50% of NMEs faced initial FDA rejection, with a notable portion (about half of those rejected) eventually gaining approval after a median delay exceeding one year—ultimately resulting in a 73% overall approval rate when accounting for resubmissions.

The analysis identified the most common rejection rationales: dose selection uncertainties (15.9%), flawed or clinically unmeaningful endpoints (13.2%), inconsistent results across endpoints (13.2%), inconsistent results across trials/sites (11.3%), and insufficient efficacy versus standard of care (13.2%). A critical distinction emerged between permanently rejected and delayed-approval candidates: 76% of never-approved drugs failed on efficacy grounds, nearly double the rate observed among eventually-approved drugs, whereas safety concerns appeared roughly similar between both groups. In essence, inadequate efficacy appeared to represent a fatal flaw, while safety issues were more amenable to resolution.

Subsequent developments have both validated and complicated the article's observations. The 2010-2020 period saw significant FDA policy shifts, notably the 21st Century Cures Act (2016), which expanded expedited pathways, breakthrough therapy designations, and accelerated approval mechanisms. These changes substantially altered the regulatory landscape.

Post-2014 data reveals that FDA approval rates have generally trended upward, with some years exceeding 80% for novel drugs, though this varies considerably by therapeutic area. The rise of precision medicine, orphan drugs, and novel modalities (gene therapies, cell therapies, and increasingly sophisticated biologics) has introduced new regulatory complexities. Oncology drugs, in particular, have benefited from more flexible evidentiary standards and surrogate endpoints, contributing to heightened approval rates in that domain.

However, the fundamental challenges around clinical trial design, endpoint selection, and demonstration of meaningful clinical benefit persist. The period also witnessed several high-profile drug withdrawals and controversies (e.g., Aduhelm's controversial Alzheimer's approval), highlighting ongoing tensions between accelerated access and evidentiary rigor.

Post-2014 developments revealed the growing importance of manufacturing quality, real-world evidence requirements, and post-marketing study commitments—factors not prominently featured in the original analysis.
