# Sensitized Redox Photochemistry - And Some Opinions (June 2017)

## 1. SUMMARY

The article discusses a new sensitized photoredox chemistry system from a Regensburg research group. Rather than having a single ruthenium complex both absorb light and perform electron transfer, this approach separates the roles: a ruthenium complex harvests the light and transfers energy to a polycyclic hydrocarbon (such as anthracene or pyrene), which then carries out the redox reaction with the substrate. This energy-transfer architecture enables access to much higher redox potentials than single-component systems, expanding the scope of possible transformations.

The authors successfully demonstrated C-C bond formations using aryl halides with N-methylpyrrole as trapping agents in good yields, including challenging substrates like aryl chlorides and aryl triflates that are typically less reactive. The system showed efficient energy transfer kinetics—luminescence quenching was faster than competing electron transfer with tertiary amines—which is essential for selectivity.

The second half of the article addresses the broader community reception of photoredox chemistry, noting recurring hostile commentary that questions the novelty and practical value of such work. The author contrasts this negativity with the enthusiastic adoption they've observed among colleagues in medicinal and synthetic chemistry settings, suggesting that critics may represent a vocal minority rather than mainstream opinion.

This includes work with organic sensitizers, iridium complexes, and other combinations, enabling reactions that were previously inaccessible.

By 2020-2024, photoredox chemistry was routinely taught in graduate curricula and represented a mature toolbox rather than an exotic technique. The "hostile" commentary referenced in the article largely subsided as the field demonstrated concrete value in pharmaceutical synthesis and materials chemistry applications. Major chemical suppliers now carry diverse families of photocatalysts, indicating market validation.
