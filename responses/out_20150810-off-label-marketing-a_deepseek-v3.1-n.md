## 1. SUMMARY
The article discusses the legal challenges to FDA regulations prohibiting "off-label" drug promotion. Historically, FDA has restricted pharmaceutical companies from promoting drugs for uses beyond those specifically approved and listed on the official drug label, with violations leading to billion-dollar fines. The legal landscape began shifting in 2012 when a federal appeals court ruled that off-label promotion constituted protected commercial speech under the First Amendment. In August 2015, the district court in *Amarin v. FDA* granted a preliminary injunction, ruling that Amarin (maker of the fish-oil prescription drug Vascepa) could promote its product off-label as long as it made "truthful statements." This legal trend suggested a fundamental shift away from FDA pre-approval control toward post-communication standards of scientific accuracy, though implementation was uncertain due to ambiguities in defining "truthful" statements, particularly regarding medication omissions and misleading citations.
