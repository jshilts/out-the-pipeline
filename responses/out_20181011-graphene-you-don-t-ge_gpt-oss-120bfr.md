# Graphene: You Don't Get What You Pay For (Oct 2018)

## 1. SUMMARY  
The 2018 commentary warned that the commercial graphene market was a “mess.”  An analysis of 60 suppliers showed that most products marketed as graphene were in fact graphene oxide, reduced graphene oxide, or multilayer graphite‑like material.  Only a handful of vendors delivered material with > 10 % true monolayer graphene, and the average product corresponded to roughly four stacked layers—far from the ideal single‑atom sheet.  The authors argued that this poor‑quality supply chain explains why graphene‑based technologies have not yet become commonplace, and they urged researchers to verify the material they purchase rather than trust vendor labels.

## 2. HISTORY

ISO/TC 229 released ISO 27630 (2020) defining terminology and measurement methods, and ASTM published a suite of standards (e.g., ASTM D7264‑20 for graphene‑based composites, ASTM D7619‑21 for graphene oxide).  Commercial vendors began offering “certified graphene” with third‑party analytical reports (Raman spectroscopy, X‑ray photoelectron spectroscopy, AFM thickness measurements).  While these standards improved transparency, they did not eliminate low‑purity bulk‑graphite products that continue to be sold under the “graphene” label, especially in low‑cost bulk markets.

**Market evolution** – Global graphene revenue grew from ≈ $200 M in 2018 to about $1.2 B in 2024 (multiple market‑research reports).  The growth was driven largely by **few‑layer graphene** (2–10 layers) used in conductive inks, polymer composites, thermal interface materials, and electromagnetic‑interference (EMI) shielding.  True monolayer graphene, required for high‑mobility electronic devices, remained a niche, high‑cost commodity (>$100 / g) supplied by a small set of specialty producers (e.g., Graphenea, 2D‑Materials, XG Sciences).  

**Commercial applications** –  
* **Conductive inks & printed electronics** – Companies such as **Versarien**, **Haydale**, and **NanoGraf** launched graphene‑based inks that are now used in RFID tags, flexible sensors, and low‑cost prototyping.  Performance is comparable to silver nanowire inks but at lower material cost and with better environmental stability.  
* **Energy storage** – Graphene‑enhanced lithium‑ion and sodium‑ion batteries entered limited production (e.g., **Skeleton Technologies**’ graphene‑based ultracapacitors).  The improvements are modest (≈ 10–20 % energy density boost) and the products are marketed as “graphene‑enhanced” rather than pure graphene electrodes.  
* **Thermal management** – Graphene‑filled thermal interface pads and pastes (e.g., **GrafTech**, **LG Chem**) have been adopted in high‑performance computing and electric‑vehicle power electronics.  These rely on few‑layer graphene flakes that provide high in‑plane conductivity while being inexpensive to produce.  

**Failed or stalled projects** – Several high‑profile attempts to commercialize monolayer‑graphene transistors, photodetectors, or flexible displays have been discontinued or scaled back.  The primary obstacles cited were (1) difficulty in producing wafer‑scale monolayer graphene with low defect density, and (2) lack of reproducible transfer processes compatible with existing semiconductor fabs.  Notable examples include the shutdown of **IBM’s graphene‑based logic research** (2021) and the pivot of **Samsung’s graphene‑interconnect program** toward copper‑alloy alternatives (2022).

**Business outcomes** – A handful of early‑stage graphene companies (e.g., **Graphene Frontiers**, **Cambridge Graphene**) either merged with larger materials firms or exited the market.  Conversely, larger chemical manufacturers (e.g., **BASF**, **Arkema**) incorporated graphene‑filled composites into their product lines, leveraging their existing distribution channels.

**Policy and funding** – Government programs that once poured billions into “graphene hype” (e.g., the EU Graphene Flagship, US DOE Graphene Initiative) have shifted focus toward “graphene‑enabled technologies” rather than pure graphene production.  Funding now emphasizes integration of few‑layer graphene into existing products, standardization, and life‑cycle assessment.
