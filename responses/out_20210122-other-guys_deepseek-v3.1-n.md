## 1. SUMMARY

This commentary argues that lipids and carbohydrates are underappreciated classes of biomolecules compared to the dominant focus on proteins and nucleic acids in both research and popular understanding. The author explains that while proteins and nucleic acids receive disproportionate attention due to DNA's coding role and protein synthesis, lipids and carbohydrates play equally fundamental biological roles: lipids form cellular membranes, serve as energy sources, and provide hormone precursors, while carbohydrates drive cellular metabolism, regulate protein localization through glycosylation, enable immune recognition, and form structural components of nucleic acids.

The core thesis is that this neglect stems from technological limitations rather than biological insignificance. Carbohydrates present exceptional structural complexity—far exceeding proteins—with multiple linkage possibilities, anomeric configurations, and varied ring structures that make both synthesis and sequencing extremely challenging. Unlike automated synthesis available for proteins and nucleic acids, carbohydrate chemistry requires highly specific, case-by-case approaches. Lipids face similar characterization difficulties, being challenging to separate chromatographically and distinguish via NMR due to similar physical properties that nonetheless have distinct biological significance (e.g., nutritional differences between fatty acid variants).
