## 2. HISTORY
The Nature reproducibility initiative was part of a broader movement recognizing and addressing what became widely termed the "reproducibility crisis" in science. In the decade following 2013:

- **Journal Policy Evolution**: Most major scientific publishers adopted similar reproducibility guidelines. PLOS journals strengthened data availability requirements, and many publishers implemented mandatory data deposition in repositories and detailed statistical checklists.

- **Research Culture and Infrastructure**: Major initiatives emerged including the NIH rigor and reproducibility guidelines (2014-2016), the Center for Open Science's Transparency and Openness Promotion (TOP) guidelines (2014), and numerous institutional reproducibility training programs.

- **Statistical and Reporting Standards**: Reporting checklists became widespread (CONSORT for clinical trials, ARRIVE for animal studies, and others). Pre-registration of studies grew significantly, reducing publication bias and questionable research practices.

- **Data Sharing Platforms**: Repositories like Gene Expression Omnibus, ClinicalTrials.gov, and institutional data archives became standard infrastructure, supported by funder mandates (NIH 2003 data sharing policy strengthened in 2020; NSF and other major funders followed).

- **Business Implications**: The reproducibility movement did not lead to FDA approvals or clinical failures directly—it was a methodological and cultural reform rather than a therapeutic intervention. However, it did impact research practices across pharmaceutical companies, which increasingly adopted more stringent internal validation standards and registered more trials prospectively.

- **Ongoing Challenges**: Meta-analyses through the late 2010s and early 2020s continued showing substantial non-reproducibility rates across fields, indicating that policy changes alone did not solve the underlying systemic issues.
