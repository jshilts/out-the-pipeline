# Single-Cell Compound Measurements - Now In A Real Animal (June 2014)

## 1. SUMMARY

This commentary discusses a follow-up study to earlier single-cell pharmacokinetics work on olaparib, a PARP1 inhibitor. The technique uses fluorescence polarization/anisotropy microscopy with a BODIPY-tagged version of olaparib. By exploiting the different rotational behavior of bound versus unbound fluorescent molecules, researchers could distinguish the drug's distribution within individual cells and determine the extent of specific binding to the nuclear PARP1 target.

The key advance highlighted in this 2014 article was the extension of single-cell measurements from cultured cells to live animals. Using a window chamber implanted over a xenograft in mice, the researchers successfully demonstrated the same dynamics: rapid cellular uptake of the labeled drug followed by binding to nuclear PARP1 that persisted even as unbound fluorescence cleared. The author identifies this as the first such real-time, single-cell drug tracking in a living animal. The article briefly references an alternative whole-cell thermal shift assay method that doesn't require compound modification.

The fluorescence imaging techniques did not become standard clinical tools for guiding PARP inhibitor therapy, though they continued to evolve for preclinical research.

The "window chamber" methodology demonstrated in this work (and similar intravital microscopy approaches) found a lasting niche in academic research and some pharmaceutical preclinical studies, enabling visualization of tumor biology and drug penetration. Whole-organism imaging continued advancing with higher-throughput platforms emerging over the subsequent decade. The two-photon and near-IR techniques referenced evolved but faced practical limitations for widespread clinical deployment; most clinical decision-making continues to rely on established biomarkers and genomic assays rather than real-time cellular imaging.
