## 2. HISTORY

The original 2015 study by Pisa et al. was met with considerable skepticism. Critics pointed to methodological concerns including potential contamination issues, the use of nested PCR which can amplify contaminants, and questions about whether the detected fungal material represented genuine brain infection or environmental contamination during sample processing. 

Subsequent research efforts have largely failed to replicate these findings. The Alzheimer's research field has instead consolidated around other major hypotheses, particularly the amyloid hypothesis (though this has faced its own challenges), tau pathology, neuroinflammation, and genetic factors like APOE4. Clinical trials testing antifungal treatments for Alzheimer's have been extremely limited and have not demonstrated significant benefits. The medical community has remained skeptical, partly due to the fact that many Alzheimer's patients receive various medications over their disease course without notable antifungal-related improvements.

By 2020-2023, the fungal hypothesis remained a marginal theory in Alzheimer's research, with most major funding and research efforts focused on amyloid, tau, and other established pathways.
