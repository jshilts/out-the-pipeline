The subsequent decade revealed NASH as one of biotech's most challenging and expensive graveyards, with **Intercept's story becoming a cautionary tale for drug development hype cycles**. **2015-2016:** The FDA issued a Complete Response Letter for NASH, requiring additional safety and efficacy data despite positive trial results.

**2020:** After spending years and hundreds of millions conducting additional trials, Intercept's OCA failed to gain FDA approval for NASH, dealing a major blow to the FXR agonist approach. The stock, which once touched $445, fell to around $20-30.

**2023:** Intercept was acquired by Italian pharmaceutical company Alfasigma for approximately $800 million, a far cry from its peak $8+ billion valuation during the 2014 frenzy.

**The broader NASH landscape proved equally brutal.** Major pharmaceutical companies poured billions into NASH research only to see multiple late-stage failures. Genfit's Elafibranor failed in 2020, Gilead's various combination therapies failed to show sufficient efficacy, Allergan's cenicriviroc program was terminated, and numerous other approaches failed to demonstrate meaningful clinical benefit without significant safety concerns.

**The fundamental challenges** remained: lack of reliable non-invasive biomarkers, uncertain disease mechanism, patient heterogeneity, and the FDA's strict requirement for hard clinical outcomes rather than tissue markers. Several companies pivoted to developing non-invasive diagnostic tools and combination therapies, with limited success.

By the early 2020s, the NASH field had become characterized by **widespread disappointment and consolidation**, with many investors losing substantial capital and companies abandoning programs.
