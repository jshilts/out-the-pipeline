The 2014 article reported on groundbreaking research by Ron Evans and colleagues at the Salk Institute demonstrating that treatment with fibroblast growth factor 1 (FGF1) appeared to reverse type II diabetes in mice. The authors showed that repeated therapy increased insulin sensitivity and suppressed hepatic glucose production, suggesting FGF1 could be "the Real Mechanism behind the disease."

The article highlighted that this builded on earlier findings that FGF1 knockout mice developed severe glucose control problems on high-fat diets. ## 2. HISTORY

**2014-2017: Initial Validation and Mechanistic Deepening**
Following the original publication, multiple research groups confirmed FGF1's remarkable metabolic effects in animal models. Researchers showed that FGF1 administration reduced glucose levels and improved insulin sensitivity across various diabetic mouse models (Schleicher et al., 2015; Dutchak et al., 2015). The scientific community rapidly expanded understanding of FGF1's mechanism, demonstrating its effects on adipose tissue remodeling, hypothalamic regulation, and mitochondrial function.

**2018-2020: Translation Challenges Emerge**
As preclinical work progressed, several significant challenges became apparent. First, the protein's rapid clearance (short half-life) required frequent administration or pharmaceutical modification. Second, FGF1's mitogenic properties raised concerns about cancer risks, particularly given that FGFR1 signaling is involved in several malignancies. Third, the doses required for metabolic effects in larger animals appeared higher than initially hoped, complicating manufacturing and cost considerations.

**2021-2024: Clinical Translation Stalls**
Despite promising preclinical results, FGF1 has not progressed to human clinical trials for diabetes treatment. Several factors contributed to this plateau:
- Competition from highly effective GLP-1 receptor agonists (semaglutide, tirzepatide) that achieved unprecedented weight loss and glucose control
- Development of other promising metabolic targets (FGF21 analogs, bile acid modulators)
- Persistence of concerns about proliferative side effects
- High development costs without clear differentiation from existing therapies

**Parallel Research Directions**
Interestingly, FGF1 research pivoted toward other applications. Studies explored FGF1's potential in treating metabolic dysfunction-associated steatotic liver disease (MASLD) and acute kidney injury. Some groups investigated FGF1 mimetics and small molecule FGFR1 activators as alternatives to the protein itself.
