# Qi Gong and Placebo Effects (January 2009)

## 1. SUMMARY

This 2009 commentary addresses Qi Gong practices in response to criticism the author received after commenting on Deepak Chopra and related alternative medicine proponents. The author distinguishes between two definitions of Qi Gong: (1) low-impact exercise programs derived from Chinese traditions, which they consider legitimate, and (2) practices claiming to manipulate "universal healing energies" or "energy fields," which they view as unsupported by evidence. 

The central argument focuses on the need for empirical testing when effectiveness claims are made. The author acknowledges exercise has clear benefits but challenges specific Qi Gong efficacy claims, noting that existing studies tend to be small and poorly controlled. A major theme is the confounding role of placebo effects, particularly problematic when measuring outcomes like mood, blood pressure, and stress responses that are highly susceptible to belief-based improvements. The author proposes a "Don Ki Kong" protocol (a deliberately fake-sounding name) to test whether purported benefits stem from the specific practices or simply from placebo effects.
