## 2. HISTORY

The Huygens probe's landing on Titan on January 14, 2005, marked humanity's first landing in the outer solar system and remains the farthest landing from Earth ever accomplished. The mission was indeed successful: Huygens transmitted data for approximately 2 hours and 27 minutes during its descent and continued sending information from Titan's surface for about 90 minutes after landing.

Key scientific discoveries from Huygens revealed a surface with methane lakes, hydrocarbon rivers, and a complex organic chemistry that some scientists compare to early Earth conditions. The probe captured images showing a landscape with drainage channels and evidence of liquid methane precipitation - essentially rivers and lakes of liquid natural gas.

The Cassini spacecraft, which carried Huygens to the Saturn system, continued orbiting Saturn until September 2017, when it was intentionally crashed into Saturn to prevent potential contamination of Titan's or Enceladus's potentially habitable environments. Cassini made numerous flybys of Titan throughout its mission, mapping the moon's surface and studying its atmosphere and methane cycle.

Since 2005, only a handful of new planetary landings have occurred: NASA's Phoenix Mars Lander (2008), Curiosity rover (2012), InSight lander (2018), and Perseverance rover (2021), plus China's Chang'e missions to the Moon, with Chang'e 3 (2013) and Chang'e 4 (2019) achieving successful lunar landings. No other planetary body beyond Earth, the Moon, Mars, Venus, and Titan has been landed upon, confirming the article's implicit point about the rarity of such achievements.
