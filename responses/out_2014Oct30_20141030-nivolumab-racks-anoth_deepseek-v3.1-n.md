## 2. HISTORY

The subsequent decade proved the article's enthusiasm to be exceptionally well-founded. Nivolumab (Opdivo) received FDA approval for metastatic squamous non-small cell lung cancer in March 2015, just months after this article. It was the first PD-1 inhibitor approved for lung cancer, launching a wave of checkpoint inhibitor approvals.

By 2024, PD-1/PD-L1 inhibitors have become cornerstone therapies across numerous cancer types: melanoma, renal cell carcinoma, Hodgkin lymphoma, head and neck cancers, bladder cancer, and many others. The market has expanded to include pembrolizumab (Keytruda), atezolizumab (Tecentriq), durvalumab (Imfinzi), and avelumab (Bavencio), among others.

Perhaps most significantly, combination therapies proved even more powerful than monotherapy. The combination of nivolumab + ipilimumab (a CTLA-4 inhibitor) demonstrated unprecedented efficacy in melanoma and other cancers. Pembrolizumab became a first-line therapy for many cancers with specific biomarkers like high tumor mutational burden or PD-L1 expression.

The immunotherapy revolution did extend beyond checkpoint inhibitors to include CAR-T therapies (Kymriah, Yescarta) approved in 2017, and emerging modalities like BiTEs (bispecific T-cell engagers) and cancer vaccines. However, checkpoint inhibitors remain the backbone of modern immuno-oncology.
