The article discusses a landmark paper by Steve Ley and colleagues demonstrating the most ambitious application of flow chemistry to total synthesis at the time. ## 2. HISTORY

Looking back from 2024, flow chemistry has indeed become an increasingly important tool in both academic and industrial synthetic chemistry, though its adoption has been more nuanced than simple replacement of batch methods. The 2014 Ley paper represented a proof-of-concept that helped catalyze broader interest. Over the past decade, flow chemistry has found particular success in pharmaceutical and fine chemical manufacturing, where its advantages in heat transfer, safety (especially for hazardous reactions), reproducibility, and scalability are most pronounced. The technology has evolved to incorporate inline purification, real-time monitoring, and increasingly sophisticated automation.

However, the initial enthusiasm for all-inclusive flow syntheses has moderated somewhat. While flow chemistry has become standard for many types of transformations (especially those requiring extreme temperatures/pressures, or producing hazardous intermediates), complex multi-step natural product syntheses have largely remained hybrid approaches. This reflects exactly the pattern Lowe noted: flow works excellently for linear sequences producing needed quantities of intermediates, but intricate late-stage transformations—especially those involving complex ring formations, sensitive functional groups, or unpredictable stereochemical outcomes—still benefit from the flexibility and diagnostic capability of batch methods.
