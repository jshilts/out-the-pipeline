# See You in Court? Not Likely (September 2014)

## 1. SUMMARY
This article from September 2014 discusses an early legal challenge against PubPeer, an open-source post-publication peer review website. Cancer researcher Fazlul Sarkar, an author of over 500 papers with substantial NIH funding, had approximately 50 of his papers receive critical comments on PubPeer, often pointing to potential image manipulation or inconsistencies in figures. The controversy escalated when the University of Mississippi Medical Center rescinded a job offer to Sarkar—a tenured position with a $350,000 annual salary—which his attorney attributed directly to the PubPeer postings. The article notes that Sarkar was threatening legal action to identify anonymous commenters, but suggests this would face significant legal obstacles under the Communications Decency Act of 1996, which provides broad immunity to website operators for user comments.

## 2. HISTORY
The Sarkar case became a landmark legal battle that would drag on for years and fundamentally shape the relationship between anonymous online criticism and academic freedom. Contrary to the article's prediction that the case wouldn't "get very far," Sarkar, represented by attorney Nicholas Roumel, actually succeeded in obtaining a subpoena in Michigan state court in October 2014 compelling PubPeer's operators (Brandon Stell, then operating as pseudonymous "Peer 1") to disclose the identities of anonymous commenters. Various PubPeer commenters then moved to quash the subpoena.

In 2016, the Michigan Court of Appeals reversed the lower court and blocked the subpoena, ruling that the commenters' anonymity was protected. The court found Sarkar failed to meet the legal threshold for unmasking anonymous speakers. This was a significant victory for PubPeer and represented a crucial validation of post-publication peer review platforms.

Following these legal battles, Sarkar continued his career and eventually joined the University of Mississippi Medical Center despite the earlier rescinded offer, though in a different capacity. PubPeer itself grew substantially in prominence and usage following this case, becoming a widely recognized platform within the scientific community. Post-publication peer review became more accepted and institutionalized, with many journals now formally incorporating or acknowledging such platforms.
