# Holding Back Experimental Details, With Reason (October 2013)

## 1. SUMMARY

This 2013 commentary discusses the growing tension between scientific openness and responsible information control in biomedical research. It begins by noting concerns about research reproducibility and a troubling trend: surveys showed declining willingness among scientists to share detailed protocols and data, dropping from 80% to 60% over five years—attributed to lack of incentives for sharing.

The article then focuses on a critical case where researchers made a deliberate decision *not* to fully disclose their findings. Scientists had discovered a novel botulinum neurotoxin (designated BoNT/H) from a patient with infant botulism that could not be neutralized by any existing antitoxins against known botulinum types A-G. Fearing potential bioweapon misuse, both the research team and the Journal of Infectious Diseases agreed to publish the discovery's existence but withhold the genetic sequence and strain details until an effective antitoxin could be developed and deployed. The author sees this as a justified case where the risks of full disclosure outweigh the benefits—a precedent set against the backdrop of earlier controversies over publishing modified influenza virus research.

The actual effectiveness and deployment timeline of such countermeasures remains not broadly publicized for security reasons.

**Dual-Use Research of Concern Policies Formalized**: This case occurred just before and helped catalyze the formalization of U.S. policies on "dual-use research of concern" (DURC). Federal agencies, including the CDC and NIH, established formal review processes for research with potential biosecurity risks. Studies involving pathogens like botulinum toxins underwent enhanced scrutiny for publication and data-sharing.

**Reproducibility Crisis Intensified**: The broader issue of scientific reproducibility and data-sharing became more acute. Major efforts like the NIH Data Commons, FAIR data principles, academic publisher requirements for data deposition, and platforms for protocol sharing emerged to address the sharing deficits noted in the original article. However, the fundamental tension—incentives for sharing versus concerns about misuse—persisted and evolved.

**Biotechnology Security Debates Mounted**: Following this botulinum case and the 2012 H5N1 influenza virus publication controversies, biosecurity considerations increasingly influenced publication norms. Journals adopted stricter review for studies presenting potential misuse risks. In parallel, as DNA synthesis and genetic engineering tools evolved (including CRISPR and other rapid molecular biology technologies), the balance became even more delicate.
