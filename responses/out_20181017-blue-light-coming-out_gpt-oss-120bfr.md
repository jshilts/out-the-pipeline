# Blue Light Coming Out of the NMR (Oct 2018)

## 1. SUMMARY  
The 2018 JACS Communication from Merck’s Process R&D team described a “LED‑NMR” platform in which a blue‑LED light source is mounted inside an NMR probe, allowing a photochemical reaction to be switched on and off while the reaction mixture is observed in real time. The authors applied the setup to an iron‑catalyzed cyclization of an alkynol to an exo‑enol ether (product 2a). The active catalyst is generated by photodissociation of a CO ligand from tetraphenylcyclopentadienone iron tricarbonyl; CO loss can also be induced thermally or by co‑oxidants, but those routes gave messy product mixtures.  

Key mechanistic findings were:  

* A quantum yield of ≈ 3, indicating that each photon initiates a catalytic turnover that proceeds for about three cycles before the catalyst re‑binds CO and becomes inactive.  
* The reaction continues, albeit more slowly, after the light is turned off, consistent with a “dark” catalytic phase that ends when the catalyst re‑captures CO.  
* Deprotonation of the oxygen atom and intramolecular attack on the alkyne is the turnover‑limiting step, demonstrated by a Hammett correlation across a series of aryl substituents.  
* Using ^13C‑labelled CO showed that CO released in the first photodissociation can be re‑captured, while loss of a second CO leads to irreversible deactivation. Consequently, the reaction yield improves when the reaction vessel has minimal head‑space (i.e., when dissolved CO concentration is high).  

The paper concluded with a broad substrate scope and suggested that the LED‑NMR approach could become a general tool for mechanistic studies of photochemical processes in process chemistry.
