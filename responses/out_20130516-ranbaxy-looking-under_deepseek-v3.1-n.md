In 2014, Ranbaxy reached a settlement with the FTC over anticompetitive practices related to generic drugs. The company's regulatory problems persisted, with ongoing quality control issues leading to further FDA warnings and restrictions. In April 2015, Daiichi Sankyo (which had acquired Ranbaxy in 2008 for $4.6 billion) sold its controlling stake to Sun Pharmaceutical Industries for $3.2 billion—representing a substantial loss on their original investment. The deal created India's largest pharmaceutical company.

Post-acquisition, Sun Pharma faced the challenge of resolving Ranbaxy's outstanding regulatory issues, which took several years and substantial investment in manufacturing upgrades and quality systems. The FDA gradually lifted import alerts on former Ranbaxy facilities between 2015-2018 as remediation efforts succeeded. The scandal also led to increased FDA scrutiny of Indian pharmaceutical facilities overall, with more frequent inspections and higher rejection rates for Indian generic drug applications in the subsequent years.

The broader impact included heightened awareness of supply chain vulnerabilities and quality issues in generic drugs, influencing regulatory approaches globally and affecting purchasing decisions by healthcare systems concerned about drug quality and safety.
