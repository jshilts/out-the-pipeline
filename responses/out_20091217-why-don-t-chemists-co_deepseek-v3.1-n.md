# Why Don't Chemists Communicate? (Or Do We?)

## 1. SUMMARY

This 2009 commentary reflects on why chemistry as a discipline appeared slower to adopt web-based scholarly communication tools compared to fields like physics and biology. The author references a Nature Chemistry article examining why chemistry preprint servers and data-sharing initiatives consistently failed to gain traction, while counterparts like arXiv and GenBank flourished in other fields.

The piece explores several explanatory factors: the culture of chemistry emphasizes making and characterizing physical substances rather than generating reusable datasets; chemists historically didn't share preprints as physicists did, so infrastructure alone couldn't change behavior; chemistry research is more fragmented across smaller research groups rather than large collaborative efforts requiring coordination; and various sub-disciplines have different communication needs and constraints, especially in industry where information sharing has commercial sensitivity.

The article notes failed initiatives like Elsevier's preprint server and the uncertain adoption of Nature Precedings, suggesting that successful tools need to align with existing practices rather than attempting to create entirely new behaviors. A notable observation highlights that science blogging at the time was primarily done by students, journalists, and early-career researchers, with "hardly any established scientists" maintaining blogs due to time constraints, leaving open whether this would become mainstream.
