# The Perils of Poor Equipment (January 2009)

## 1. SUMMARY

The article discusses Peter Medawar's critique of romantic narratives about researchers making great discoveries with outdated or insufficient equipment. Medawar argued that while this is technically possible, it becomes increasingly difficult as science advances and, more importantly, it fundamentally constrains how researchers think about their work. When scientists lack access to key equipment (like LC/MS machines for compound analysis), they unconsciously learn to avoid considering experiments that would require such tools, effectively censoring their own scientific imagination. This creates a "perimeter" around their research possibilities, potentially excluding important areas of inquiry. The author draws a parallel to George Orwell's argument about how corrupt language can corrupt thought. The essay acknowledges that having abundant resources doesn't guarantee productivity—some well-equipped labs produce little of value—but argues that this represents a different kind of problem than resource scarcity.
