## 2. HISTORY

The XMRV story represents one of the most significant scientific retractions and controversies in biomedical research of the past two decades. In October 2009, researchers from the Whittemore Peterson Institute, Cleveland Clinic, and National Cancer Institute published in Science claiming that 67% of CFS patients tested positive for XMRV, compared to only 3.7% of healthy controls. This created enormous excitement within the patient community and scientific circles.

However, numerous research groups worldwide struggled to replicate these findings throughout 2010-2011. By late 2010, several major studies from respected institutions including the CDC, FDA, and NIH failed to detect XMRV in CFS patients using the same methods. The situation deteriorated rapidly when it was discovered that the positive results likely stemmed from laboratory contamination - specifically, contamination with mouse DNA from commercial laboratory reagents and cross-contamination between samples.

In September 2011, Science published a formal "Editorial Expression of Concern" regarding the original paper. By December 2011, the journal retracted the study entirely. The lead author, Judy Mikovits, was dismissed from her position at the Whittemore Peterson Institute amid allegations of insubordination and misappropriation of research materials. The criminal charges mentioned in the article related to accusations that Mikovits had taken laboratory notebooks and electronic files from her former institution.

The scientific consensus that emerged was clear: XMRV was not associated with CFS. Multiple large-scale studies definitively showed that the original findings resulted from contamination artifacts. No diagnostic tests based on XMRV were ever approved for clinical use, and no treatments targeting XMRV were developed for CFS patients. The entire research avenue proved to be a dead end, representing a substantial waste of research resources and, more significantly, raising false hopes among patients.

The episode had lasting consequences for CFS research, diverting attention and funding away from more promising research directions for several years. It also sparked important discussions about laboratory quality control, the importance of replication studies, and the need for more rigorous standards in biomedical research.
