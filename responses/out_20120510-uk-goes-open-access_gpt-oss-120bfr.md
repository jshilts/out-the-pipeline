## 2. HISTORY  
**Policy rollout (2013‑2022)**  
* **April 2013** – The Research Councils UK (RCUK) OA policy came into force, requiring all peer‑reviewed articles arising from RCUK‑funded research to be made OA either by publishing in a Gold OA journal (or hybrid journal with an APC) or by depositing the accepted manuscript in an OA repository (Green OA) within six months of publication.  
* **2014‑2018** – The policy was extended to all UK higher‑education institutions receiving block‑grant research funding (via the Higher Education Funding Council for England, Scotland, Wales, and Northern Ireland).  By 2018, ≈ 70 % of UK‑funded articles were OA, a steep rise from < 30 % in 2012.  

**Publisher response**  
* Major commercial publishers (Wiley, Elsevier, Springer Nature, Taylor & Francis) negotiated “transformative” agreements with UK consortia (e.g., Jisc, UK RI) that bundled subscription access with OA publishing rights.  These “Read‑and‑Publish” deals, first signed in 2017, shifted large portions of UK research spending from subscription fees to APCs while preserving access to legacy content.  
* APC levels in the UK have been a point of contention.  Average APCs for Gold OA in the UK rose from ~£1,500 in 2014 to ~£2,500–£3,000 by 2021, prompting the UK RI to set a ceiling of £2,000 for most UK‑funded projects in 2022.  

**Infrastructure and compliance**  
* The UK established **UK PubMed Central** (now part of Europe PMC) as the national repository for Green OA deposits.  Compliance monitoring tools (e.g., the “Open Access Dashboard”) were introduced, and non‑compliant institutions face funding penalties.  
* The policy spurred the growth of institutional OA support offices, and many universities now have dedicated OA funds to cover APCs for their researchers.  

**Impact on the publishing ecosystem**  
* The proportion of OA articles worldwide grew, with the UK becoming one of the largest OA spenders (≈ £200 M / yr by 2022).  
* Subscription revenues for commercial publishers from UK institutions have declined modestly, but the shift to APC‑based models has largely offset the loss.  
* No major “collapse” of the traditional publishing model has occurred; instead, a hybrid landscape of subscription, Gold OA, and Green OA now dominates UK scholarly communication.
