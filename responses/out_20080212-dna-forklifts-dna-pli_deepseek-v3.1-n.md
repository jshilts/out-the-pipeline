# DNA Forklifts, DNA Pliers (February 2008)

## 1. SUMMARY

This 2008 article describes a breakthrough in DNA nanotechnology where researchers demonstrated the ability to manipulate individual molecules using DNA as both "nanoscale pliers" and "nano-Velcro." A Munich research team published work in Science showing they could use DNA strands to create a molecular "depot" on a chip surface, with complementary DNA strands attached to target molecules.

The technique employed an atomic force microscope (AFM) tip equipped with DNA strands that could "pick up" molecules from the depot through DNA hybridization. Through careful engineering, the DNA binding had different strengths - one strand would unzip "base-by-base like a zipper" while another would shear off all at once. This allowed researchers to precisely control when molecules detached and reattached. The team successfully demonstrated moving fluorescent molecules from one location to another on the chip, with about one-third of attempts successfully picking up single molecules. While the process was delicate and the DNA tags were relatively large compared to the target molecules, this represented a significant proof-of-concept for programmable molecular manipulation.
