# The Real Numbers on Tamiflu (May 2014)

## 1. SUMMARY
The article discusses the controversy surrounding Tamiflu (oseltamivir), an antiviral medication marketed by Roche. The Cochrane Collaboration had initially published a 2008 review suggesting modest benefits, but later discovered that this positive conclusion relied heavily on a single paper summarizing ten clinical studies—eight of which had never been published. This triggered a multi-year effort by Cochrane researchers to obtain the complete clinical trial data from Roche.

The article describes Roche's obstructive behavior: demanding confidentiality agreements that would prevent transparency, making excuses about data sharing, and generally withholding information—conduct characterized as "shameful" and contributing to the pharmaceutical industry's poor public reputation. When the data finally emerged, the Cochrane analysis found Tamiflu to be largely unimpressive: it doesn't reduce hospitalizations, has unclear effects on mortality, shows questionable benefits for pneumonia, and only marginally shortens flu symptom duration by hours. The author notes that Tamiflu's main drawback is its narrow window of effectiveness—it works best when taken after infection but before symptoms appear, making it impractical for real-world use.

- **AllTrials Campaign**: This Cochrane-led episode strengthened the global AllTrials initiative demanding that all clinical trial results be registered and published.

**Continued Regulatory Approval and Usage**: Despite the Cochrane review's negative findings, Tamiflu maintained regulatory approval worldwide and continued to be stockpiled extensively during flu seasons and pandemic preparations. The WHO kept Tamiflu on its essential medicines list for years following the controversy.

The Tamiflu experience influenced more critical evaluation of COVID-19 therapeutic claims.

**Economic Impact**: The controversy had relatively minor impact on Roche's financial performance, as Tamiflu remained a significant revenue generator, particularly during seasonal flu outbreaks and pandemic scares.
